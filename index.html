About Me Resume Research Personal Interests

By pursuing interests that span both technology and medicine, I have learned to integrate analytical thinking with human-centered care. My approach to inquiry is inspired by Dr. Carol Greider, who, when discussing the discovery of telomerase, emphasized the serendipity of basic research.

"What intrigues basic scientists like me is that any time we do a series of experiments, there are going to be three or four new questions that come up when you think you’ve answered one. Our approach shows that while you can do research that tries to answer specific questions about a disease, you can also just follow your nose."

As a student studying Psychology and Data Science, I am particularly interested in how empirical data can inform actionable psychiatric solutions. While the human mind is inherently complex, tools from data science—such as predictive modeling and longitudinal analysis—offer powerful ways to identify trends and uncover biological markers that can guide more effective, personalized treatments. This commitment to the scientific process requires a deep, almost intuitive connection to the data, as Nobel Laureate Dr. Barbara McClintock famously observed:

"It never occurred to me that there was going to be any stumbling block. Not that I had the answer, but [I had] the joy of going at it. When you have that joy, you do the right experiments. You let the material tell you where to go, and it tells you at every step what the next has to be because you're integrating with an overall brand new pattern in mind."

Looking ahead, I hope to apply this same level of curiosity and tenacity to my own research, working to improve clinical outcomes and bridge the gap between empirical data and human-centered psychiatric care.

"The art and science of asking the right questions is the source of all knowledge." — Thomas Berger
