Welcome

I’m Prerana Kulla, a freshman at UNC–Chapel Hill from Harrisburg, NC. Before coming to Carolina, I graduated from the North Carolina School of Science and Mathematics in Morganton. Currently, I am pursuing a double major in Psychology and Data Science with a minor in Business of Health. I aim to use the diverse foundation I build during my undergraduate years to advance psychiatric research within women’s health.

Growing up, having my grandparents across the world meant our bond was not as close as it could have been. When my grandmother was diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease, the guilt of not spending enough meaningful time with her before the disease claimed her memories deeply tested my resilience. After her passing, I realized that my lack of confrontation had limited our relationship, motivating me to ensure others would not feel as helpless as I once did when facing Alzheimer’s. I took action by joining the Youth Movement Against Alzheimer’s (YMAA), focusing on understanding her condition rather than trying to reinvent solutions. By founding a school chapter, I formed partnerships with senior homes, introduced brain anatomy through hands-on dissections, and organized Brain Bee competitions. As a board member, I amplified the voices of adolescents across the country who struggled to connect with loved ones affected by early-onset Alzheimer’s. Although I could not change the past, I learned to channel my experiences into meaningful impact, which ultimately drew me toward neuroscience.

Building on this experience, I established the Menstrual Movement to address period poverty. After organizing donation drives in both Harrisburg, NC—my hometown—and India, I recognized a critical need in Western North Carolina following Hurricane Helene. The Menstrual Movement in Morganton resulted in a successful community-wide drive that supported over 500 women and led to the publication of The Red Ribbon, a children’s book designed to amplify advocacy narratives and destigmatize reproductive health education.

At UNC–Chapel Hill, I continue to balance my interdisciplinary interests. During my freshman year, I served as a Residential Computing Consultant (RCC) with ResNET, assisting students with network connectivity issues, activating Ethernet ports, and supporting residential programming needs. Moving forward, I will take on additional responsibilities as a Senior Residential Computing Consultant (SRCC), including mentoring RCCs, assigning technical tickets, and leading staff meetings. Beyond ResNET, I serve as the Volunteering Coordinator for NEXGENCEO, a student organization focused on enhancing business literacy for K–12 students through initiatives like the annual Business Fair in partnership with Kenan-Flagler. Additionally, I am the Vice President of External Relations for the Technology Consulting Group, where I build partnerships with organizations and gain hands-on consulting experience that complements my technical background. I am also an active member of Carolina Women in Business, CPALS, and Bharat Sangeet. As a Research Assistant in the Schiller Lab at the Center for Women’s Mood Disorders, I contribute to participant recruitment and screening, as well as data collection involving brain imaging and blood sampling. Our research examines neural and molecular responses to reward in women, a process critical to understanding mental health mechanisms.

By pursuing interests that span both technology and medicine, I have learned to integrate analytical thinking with human-centered care. My approach to inquiry is inspired by Dr. Carol Greider, who, when discussing the discovery of telomerase, emphasized the serendipity of basic research.

"What intrigues basic scientists like me is that any time we do a series of experiments, there are going to be three or four new questions that come up when you think you’ve answered one. Our approach shows that while you can do research that tries to answer specific questions about a disease, you can also just follow your nose."

As a student studying Psychology and Data Science, I am particularly interested in how empirical data can inform actionable psychiatric solutions, bringing a quantitative lens to mental health. While the human mind is inherently complex, tools from data science—such as predictive modeling, pattern recognition, and longitudinal analysis—offer powerful ways to identify trends and uncover biological markers that can guide more effective and personalized treatments. This commitment to the scientific process requires a deep, almost intuitive connection to the data, as Dr. Barbara McClintock observed:

"It never occurred to me that there was going to be any stumbling block. Not that I had the answer, but [I had] the joy of going at it. When you have that joy, you do the right experiments. You let the material tell you where to go, and it tells you at every step what the next has to be because you're integrating with an overall brand new pattern in mind."

Looking ahead, I hope to apply the same rigor of curiosity into my own research, working to improve clinical outcomes and bridge the gap between empirical data and human-centered psychiatric care.
