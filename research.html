Research Statement

My research agenda focuses on exploring the neurobiological mechanisms underlying psychiatric and affective disorders in adolescent girls, specifically examining how the pubertal surge in estradiol influences neural reward circuitry and emotional reactivity.

Although clinical studies have identified and defined a rise in the prevalence of anxiety during early adolescence, the specific temporal link between rapid pubertal development and the remodeling of the mesolimbic dopamine pathway remains poorly understood.

My future research goals aim to bridge this delta by utilizing longitudinal functional neuroimaging and hormonal profiling to determine how varying pubertal trajectories correlate with heightened neural sensitivity to rewards in day-to-day life.

By identifying the critical neural windows where hormonal surges impact emotional development the most, this research seeks to facilitate the development of early-intervention models that can mitigate the progression of psychiatric disorders in adolescent girls before they become chronic.

Sustainable Menstrual Hygiene Materials

April 2024 - March 2025

Focus: Biomaterials, Sustainability, Global Health

Investigated pineapple and sisal fibers as biodegradable, localized alternatives to cotton for menstrual pads. This research utilized enzymatic and chemical delignification methods to enhance absorbent properties, aiming to address period poverty in resource-limited regions through decentralized production.

Key Findings: Treated sisal fibers achieved absorbency levels competitive with traditional cotton, reaching an average ratio of 112.5 grams fluid/grams square.

Biodegradability of Cellulose in Filter Paper

January 2024 - April 2024

Focus: Environmental Science, Mycology, Waste Management

Evaluated how various ink formulations—water, algae, alcohol, and oil-based—impact the breakdown of cellulose substrates by specialized fungi. I quantified enzyme activity and substrate degradation through spectrophotometer experiments.

Key Findings: Water- and algae-based inks showed significant degradation comparable to control samples, whereas oil- and alcohol-based inks were found to inhibit the natural degradation process.
